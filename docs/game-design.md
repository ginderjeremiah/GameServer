# Battle Mechanics

Battling is the core mechanic of the game, and it is designed to be a continuous and engaging experience for players. Players will idle in different zones, automatically fighting monsters that spawn in those zones. During battle players and enemies will automatically use their skills, until one side is defeated or a timeout is reached (or the player changes zones). Players will earn experience points and progress towards Challenges as they battle.

It is important to note that the game is designed to replicate battles on both the frontend and backend as an anti-cheat measure. The backend is the source of truth for all game logic and state, but the frontend also simulates battles in real-time to provide a responsive and engaging user experience. This means that all battle logic must be implemented in both the frontend and backend, the results must be consistent between the two, and the backend must be able to simulate the battle very quickly (performance matters significantly there).

There are some choices already made in the design of the game to support this:

- All battle RNG MUST use the same implementation (currently Mulberry32) that can be seeded with the same value on both the frontend and backend to ensure consistent results.
- The "state" of a player is snapshot when combat starts. This means that all relevant player stats, items, and skills are included in the combat state, and no external factors will influence the result of the battle once it has started. Since the backend does not simulate the battle until AFTER it is resolved in the frontend, this ensures that the battle will be consistent regardless of any changes that may occur to the player's state during combat.
- The battle simulation "runs" in 40ms ticks, meaning that all actions are processed in discrete intervals of 40ms. This allows for a consistent simulation and is a reasonable approach for a performant simulation on the backend while still providing a responsive experience on the frontend. The frontend should contain additional logic to interpolate values between ticks for smoother animations, but the core battle logic will be processed in these discrete ticks.

# Character Progression and Attributes

Character progression in the game is primarily driven by leveling up and allocating stat points. As players defeat monsters and complete challenges, they earn experience points (XP) that contribute to their character's level. When a player levels up, they receive stat points that can be allocated to core Attributes such as Strength, Agility, Intellect, etc.

Attributes are somewhat of a work-in-progress feature, but the general idea is that they will provide various bonuses and effects that influence combat, other Attributes, and potentially other mechanics in the game. Currently they come from stat allocations and equipment, but this will be expanded in the future. For example, a Skill may receive a damage bonus based on the player's Strength Attribute, and the player's MaxHealth Attribute will receive a bonus based on their Strength as well. Attributes are designed to go above and beyond the traditional RPG stats and provide a system for complex effects.

The attribute system is the substrate for timed skill effects (see [Skills](#skills)): applying an effect adds a real attribute modifier to the target mid-battle and expiry removes it, so derived attributes cascade naturally (a temporary Strength buff also raises MaxHealth, exactly like a permanent one). Damage-over-time and heal-over-time are realized the same way, through **per-second** attributes — the typed DoT accumulators (`BleedDamagePerSecond` / `PoisonDamagePerSecond` / `BurnDamagePerSecond`) and the typeless `HealthRegenPerSecond` — that an end-of-tick simulator phase consumes (a poison is then pure data: a debuff that adds `PoisonDamagePerSecond` to the opponent for a duration). See [Skill Effects](#skill-effects) for the DoT/HoT runtime rules.

## Attribute value conventions

Percentage- and multiplier-style attributes are stored so they are consumed **without transformation** in the battle math:

- **Percentage attributes** are stored as decimal fractions (`0.05` = 5%) and used as-is — a chance is compared directly against a `[0,1)` RNG draw — and rendered scaled ×100 (`0.05` → `5%`).
- **Multiplier attributes** carry a base ≥ 1 and are read directly as the multiplier. `CooldownRecovery` is a base-`1` cooldown multiplier: a static `1.0` base plus `0.004·Agility + 0.001·Dexterity` (a typical AGI 20 / DEX 10 build sits at `1.09` ≈ +9% charge speed). Reading the attribute directly means a multiplicative modifier scales intuitively — a `×2` `CooldownRecovery` buff genuinely doubles charge speed rather than nudging the old `1 + CDR/100` value. Like the percentage attributes, it renders scaled ×100 (`1.09` → `109%`).

This convention is the foundation the player crit/dodge attributes build on — see [Critical hits, dodge & damage reflection](#critical-hits-dodge--damage-reflection) below and the [spike](./spikes/178-player-crit-dodge-block.md).

## Mitigation: the Toughness curve

A defender's bulk mitigation is **`Toughness`**, applied as a **diminishing-returns percentage** rather than a flat subtraction (spike [#1330](./spikes/1330-mitigation-rework.md)): an incoming direct hit is multiplied by `1 − Toughness / (Toughness + K·attackerLevel)`. The key properties:

- **Effective HP is linear in Toughness** — each point is worth a constant % of effective HP, so it is never useless even as the % reduction itself diminishes.
- **The reduction asymptotes below 100%** — there is no immunity and no breakpoint; an overwhelmingly tough defender still takes a sliver of every hit.
- **`K` scales on the *attacker's* level** (`K` = `GameConstants.ToughnessMitigationConstant`, a strawman tuned during balancing), so a level-appropriate Toughness investment stays in a sensible mitigation band as both Toughness and incoming damage grow with content — fixing the flat-reduction problem where a fixed value was overpowering against small hits and worthless against big ones.

`Toughness` derives from **Endurance** (`StaticAttributeModifiers`; Agility no longer contributes — its defensive identity is Dodge). The curve applies to **both battlers** (using the attacker's level), composes **after** the damage-type resistance step, and is **bypassed by damage-over-time** (resistance is a DoT's only mitigation). This is parity-critical and mirrored frontend↔backend.

## Critical hits, dodge & damage reflection

Players can land **critical hits** (deal increased damage) and **dodge** (fully avoid an incoming attack). Enemies do **not** crit or dodge in this version — the asymmetry keeps the shared battle RNG simple and is a deliberate seam for later enemy parity. The mechanic runs in the seeded battle simulation (so the frontend and backend agree tick-for-tick; see [backend-battle.md](./backend-battle.md#battle-runtime--seeded-crit--dodge-player-only)):

- **Crit** multiplies the skill's raw damage by `CriticalDamage` (a base-≥1 multiplier read directly) **before** mitigation, so it can punch through the Toughness curve.
- **Dodge** zeroes the incoming hit entirely.

`DodgeChance` is a decimal probability (`0.05` = 5%) compared directly against the RNG draw. **Crit is a per-skill opt-in** (crit rework [#1425](https://github.com/ginderjeremiah/GameServer/issues/1425), per-skill base [#1453](https://github.com/ginderjeremiah/GameServer/issues/1453)): each `Skill` carries its own `CriticalChance` — a raw base probability, authored like `BaseDamage` and defaulting to `0` (never crits unless a designer opts it in). The battler's `CriticalChanceMultiplier` attribute only **scales** that per-skill base (`skill.CriticalChance × CriticalChanceMultiplier`) — it carries a base of `1` (like `CooldownRecovery`/`CriticalDamage`), so a committed skill still crits at its own authored rate with zero further investment, and items/proficiency/gear grant bonuses to the *multiplier* rather than flat chance, making crit a committed per-skill build identity rather than a stat every `Dexterity`/`Luck` build (or every skill) accrues for free. `CriticalDamage` keeps its `Luck` derivation and `1.5` base: inert without a crit, it pays off the moment a skill with an authored chance fires. **Dodge** stays attribute-sourced — `DodgeChance` derives from `Agility`, so evasion is live from raw allocations. Items, item-mods, and skill-effects can grant `CriticalChanceMultiplier`/`DodgeChance` through the normal modifier path; the coefficients are a conservative strawman expected to be tuned during balancing (the exact formulas live in `StaticAttributeModifiers`). Crit/dodge outcomes surface to the player in the combat log, and `CriticalChanceMultiplier`/`CriticalDamage`/`DodgeChance` appear on the attribute-breakdown screen, rendered with the percentage convention above (`CriticalDamage`'s base-`1.5` multiplier reads as `150%`). The outcomes are tallied into player **statistics** (crit/dodge counts, the crit damage dealt, and the post-mitigation damage a dodge avoided) — accumulated in the battle simulation and recorded server-side at battle completion like the other damage statistics. The player-facing **crit damage dealt** statistic is the *actual* full crit damage; the **Precision proficiency** trains on a distinct signal — the *normalized marginal crit bonus* ([#1448](#proficiency-xp--effect-based-power-normalized-accrual)) — so the displayed statistic stays intuitive while the training signal is proportional to crit-damage investment.

**Damage reflection** (spike [#1330](./spikes/1330-mitigation-rework.md)) replaced the old **Block** mechanic — a randomized flat reduction that was redundant with the Toughness curve and *anti-tank* (tanks want low variance, not RNG defence). It is the tank's **deterministic kill-condition**: on a **direct hit**, after mitigation, the defender deals `net × DamageReflection` back to the attacker, **bypassing the attacker's own mitigation**. It is deterministic (no proc), scoped to direct hits (DoT is never reflected — there is no coherent attacker for a tick), and applies in **both** directions wherever it is granted. `DamageReflection` is **authored-only** — granted by gear / item-mods / proficiency / class, never derived from a core attribute — so it is a deliberate build identity with an opportunity cost rather than a stat tax (and an enemy reflects only where a designer authors it, a free boss-design seam). Because it self-scales with the size of the hit it returns, it is strongest exactly against the hard-hitters a tank is built to absorb, converting a pure tank's otherwise-inevitable timeout draw into a win path. Its magnitude is a strawman to tune during balancing; reflected damage is booked into the player's damage-dealt / damage-taken totals.

This sharpens the **archetype split**, each niche owned by one mechanic with no overlap: **Agility → Dodge** (high-variance evasion), **Endurance → the Toughness curve** (smooth bulk) plus authored **Reflection** (offence-through-defence), and **per-skill opt-in Crit** (offensive variance — a skill's own authored chance, amplified by Precision-granted `CriticalChanceMultiplier` bonuses, with `Luck` still governing crit *damage*).

## Experience Rewards

The XP a victory awards scales with how well the enemy is matched to the player's power, so fighting something around (or above) your level pays off and grinding trivial enemies does not. The reward (`DefeatRewards`, computed server-side and sent to the client) is the enemy's attribute power times a difficulty multiplier derived from the ratio of the enemy's power to the player's: within a ±20% band the multiplier is `1`, and outside it the reward scales quadratically with the ratio (over-level enemies pay out more, under-level enemies far less).

**The quadratic multiplier is capped** so an enemy far above the player (e.g. a challenge boss cleared underleveled) can't mint a disproportionate single-battle payout — an exploitable balance cliff. The cap saturates at a power ratio of 2× (an enemy twice the player's power already pays the maximum bonus); ratios beyond that plateau rather than scaling quadratically. So the intended shape is: `1` inside the ±20% band, quadratic `ratio²` outside it, flattening to the cap at and above a 2× ratio. (The exp-reward clamps — the multiplier cap, the `int`-range floor on the raw reward, and the per-grant backstop against a tampered value — are server-only and documented in `ServerGameConstants`/`DefeatRewards`.)

"Power" on both sides is measured the same way — **the sum of the additive *core* attribute amounts** (Strength, Endurance, Intellect, Agility, Dexterity, Luck). Comparing like with like is the key invariant: derived attributes (e.g. MaxHealth) are excluded because they are computed *from* the core attributes (counting both double-counts the same investment, and a player's derived attributes are not part of their raw modifier set), and multiplicative modifiers are excluded because their amount is a scaling factor rather than a flat point total. Notably the player's measure is their **allocated** attribute total (allocations plus equipment), **not** the count of stat points they have been granted — a new player's starter allocation counts even though it was never "gained" from a level-up. The player's power is read from the **battle snapshot** frozen at battle start (the same state the battle is simulated against), not the live player aggregate, so the reward stays consistent with the fought battle and cannot be inflated by reallocating stats or unequipping gear between battle start and the victory claim.

# Skills

Skills are a fairly underdeveloped feature in the game, but the general idea is that they will provide active abilities for players that are automatically used during battles. Beyond dealing damage, a skill can carry **timed effects** — buffs/debuffs that raise or lower an attribute on the caster or the opponent for a duration (see [Skill Effects](#skill-effects)).

Skills come from the **starter kit** (new players start with their fixed starter skills equipped), **item grants** (an equipped item's `GrantedSkillId`), **proficiency milestones** (see [Proficiency](#proficiency)), and **synthesis** (combining owned skills through an authored recipe). **Challenges no longer grant skills** — the proficiency system is the skill-unlock layer, and the "beat a boss → learn a school" path routes through a skill-bearing item reward instead. An earned skill is added to the player's unlocked set **unselected** via `Player.UnlockSkill` — unlocking a skill does not auto-equip it.

Each skill carries an **acquisition classification** (`ESkillAcquisition`, a `[Flags]` set of `Player` / `Item` / `Enemy` / `Synthesis`) declaring which channels are *allowed* to grant it. It is **authoring intent**, not a record of what exists — a skill may be `Item`-flagged with no item granting it yet. The references (an item's `GrantedSkillId`, a proficiency milestone reward, an enemy's skill pool, a synthesis recipe's result) are the reality; backend authoring validation bridges them where a reference is authored (an enemy's skills must be `Enemy`-flagged; an item's `GrantedSkillId` must be `Item`-flagged; a recipe's result must be `Synthesis`-flagged). "Enemy-Only" is `Enemy` set with `Player`/`Item` clear. `Player` now means **milestone-granted** (rather than challenge-granted); starter skills are `Player`.

Each skill also carries a **rarity tier** (`ERarity`, Common→Mythic) matching items and item mods. Rarity is **authoring + display only** — neither the battle simulation nor the proficiency-XP accrual reads it, so it is purely a visual signal wherever a skill appears (the Codex, the Skills loadout screen, the battle tooltip), authored in the admin Workbench and backfilling existing skills to `Common`. (The effect-based accrual rework dropped the former per-skill rarity *tier weight*: damage already reflects a skill's power, so an explicit rarity multiplier would double-count — see [Proficiency XP](#proficiency-xp--effect-based-power-normalized-accrual).)

Each skill also carries a decorative **word of power** — the same invented arcane script a proficiency identity uses (see [Proficiency](#proficiency)), authored as a romanization plus its pronunciation and translation. It is pure flavour (never information the player must read), surfaced most prominently when a **synthesized** skill is discovered on the Synthesis screen, where the new skill's word *is* its revealed identity.

Players choose which unlocked skills are equipped (and in what order) through the skill **loadout**. The equipped set is **capped at 4** (`GameConstants.MaxSelectedSkills`, matching the enemy loadout cap) and its **order is meaningful** — it drives in-game display order and is the current (incidental) tie-break when two skills come off cooldown on the same tick. The loadout is replaced **atomically**: a single `SetSelectedSkills(orderedSkillIds)` command handles select, deselect, and reorder through one path, with the cap and unlocked-only rules enforced on the backend as **anti-cheat**. Players manage the loadout through the in-game Skills screen (see the Skills screen section in [frontend-screens.md](./frontend-screens.md#skills-screen-the-loadout-inspector)).

**Equipped items can grant an innate skill.** An item may carry an optional granted skill (see [Items](#items-item-mods-and-tags)) that is active whenever the item is equipped — **innate, additive to and exempt from the 4-cap**. The opportunity cost lives at the gear slot (you forgo a competing item's bundle), not at the loadout layer, so equipping a melee weapon that brings *Cleave* costs none of your four chosen slots. Combined with an `Item`-only acquisition flag, this is how skill–weapon coherence is enforced *implicitly* for a weapon's **signature**: an `Item`-only skill never enters the selectable pool, so the only way to field it is to equip the gear that grants it (swap weapons and it is simply gone — no greyed-out loadout slot). A **selected** weapon-typed skill is instead governed by the explicit [weapon-match gate](#weapon-match-gate) below — it stays in the loadout but greys out while its matching weapon is unequipped. The battler's skill set is assembled as **player-selected skills first (in their chosen order), then the granted skills in `EEquipmentSlot` order (Helm→Accessory), de-duplicated by id (first occurrence wins)** — so a grant that duplicates a selected skill, or another item's grant, is fielded once. The Skills screen renders the innate grants read-only alongside the loadout (surfacing the dropped duplicate as "already in your loadout").

This is a **deliberate player-favouring break in the documented player/enemy skill-count symmetry**: a player can field more than 4 skills (selected + granted), enemies cannot (they can't equip items). It is the same category of intentional asymmetry as player-only crit/dodge.

In the future, Skills will be expanded to include a wider variety of effects, such as healing and utility effects.

## Damage types

Every instance of damage carries a **leaf damage type**: `Physical`, the four elementals `Fire` / `Water` / `Earth` / `Wind`, and the DoT leaves `Bleed` / `Poison` / `Burn` (spike [#1320](./spikes/1320-damage-types.md)). A skill's **direct hit may carry several** leaf types with a split (see *Multi-typed direct hits* below); a DoT carries its type implicitly through which per-second accumulator it feeds. A leaf resolves through a static `applies()` map to the cross-cutting **category keys** it also belongs to — the four elementals roll up to `Elemental`, the three DoT leaves to `DoT`, and `Burn` is fire *and* elemental *and* DoT (a burning ember is still fire). Each key (the eight leaves plus the two categories) backs one **amplification** attribute the attacker stacks and one **resistance** attribute the defender stacks — both decimal-percentage, base 0 — so a fire hit is amplified by `FireAmplification` + `ElementalAmplification` and resisted by `FireResistance` + `ElementalResistance`. For a direct hit the type-resistance step is applied **before** the Toughness curve; for a DoT, type resistance is the *only* mitigation (DoT bypasses Toughness).

**Weapon types are damage-type leaves under `Physical`** — an additive extension of the taxonomy above (spike [#1318](./spikes/1318-proficiency-progression-avenues.md)) that lets martial masteries (Swordsmanship, Axe mastery, …) be ordinary type-routed paths parallel to the elemental ones. Each **martial** weapon leaf — `Sword`, `Axe`, `Bow`, … with an `Unarmed` default — is a leaf whose `applies()` set is `[<weapon>, Physical]`. `Physical` does **double duty** as both the generic-physical leaf *and* the shared **category key** the martial leaves roll up to, so existing `Physical` skills are unchanged. A martial skill is authored with a **fixed** weapon type (static per-skill typing, to keep its art/animation identity coherent), not one inherited from the equipped weapon. Martial leaves carry **amplification only** (the "+sword damage" build identity); there is **no per-weapon resistance**, so a weapon hit always resolves to `PhysicalResistance` through the shared key and physical resistance stays the single physical-mitigation lever. This taxonomy is what the weapon proficiency masteries route on (see [Proficiency XP](#proficiency-xp--effect-based-power-normalized-accrual)).

**A weapon's `WeaponType` is not restricted to the martial leaves — it can be any damage-type leaf.** A caster weapon declares its element directly (a Wizard's staff is a `Fire` weapon, not a new martial leaf) rather than needing a dedicated non-combat leaf. The [weapon-match gate](#weapon-match-gate) already treats non-martial types (elementals, DoT leaves) as weapon-agnostic, so a caster weapon simply fields no martial-leaf skills at all (every martial leaf mismatches it) while its elemental skills stay live across weapon swaps like any other caster kit.

**Multi-typed direct hits — a weighted split** (spike [#1343](./spikes/1343-multi-typed-damage.md)). A skill's direct hit can span more than one school: it authors a list of **weighted portions** (`{ Type, Weight }`), so a flaming sword is, say, 60% physical and 40% fire. The one raw damage number is split by weight (`raw × weight ÷ Σweights` — weights are raw, normalized at fire time, so an even split is just equal weights), and **each portion runs the ordinary single-type pipeline under its own type** (amplified by the attacker, resisted by the defender, with its own vulnerability/absorption) before the per-portion results are summed — a multi-typed hit is simply the single-typed hit run once per portion and added up. A **single** crit roll scales the whole hit and a **single** dodge zeroes it (no extra randomness from the split), and the split ratio doubles as the **cross-school proficiency contribution weight** for free: each portion trains its damage type's path in proportion to its share (see [Proficiency XP](#proficiency-xp--effect-based-power-normalized-accrual)). A single-portion skill behaves exactly like the old single-type hit. Display surfaces colour the hit by its **primary** type (the highest-weight portion). DoT is unchanged — it stays multi-typeable by authoring several effects, one per accumulator.

## Weapon-match gate

A **weapon-leaf-typed** skill (`Sword`/`Axe`/`Bow`/`Club`/`Dagger`/`Unarmed`) is fielded **only while the matching weapon is equipped** — a `Sword` skill fights only with a sword in hand, an `Axe` skill only with an axe (exact leaf-match, spike [#1342](./spikes/1342-restrict-skill-usability-by-weapon.md)). This makes the weapon slot a real loadout constraint for martial builds. **Weapon-agnostic** types — generic `Physical`, the elementals, the DoT leaves, and caster types — are **never** gated, so a hybrid kit (a sword plus two fire skills) keeps the fire skills live across weapon swaps. A multi-typed skill is gated on its **primary** (highest-weight) type.

Two properties are deliberate:

- **Non-destructive — grey-out, not auto-unequip.** A weapon swap never mutates the saved loadout (`SetSelectedSkills` is unchanged, still cap / no-duplicate / unlocked-only anti-cheat); a mismatched skill simply goes **dormant** (greyed on the Skills screen) and lights back up when a matching weapon returns. Auto-emptying a loadout on every swap would be hostile idle-game UX. The inventory also **warns before** a weapon swap that would newly dim part of the loadout.
- **Computed once at battle-loadout assembly, not per tick.** The filter is a pure function of the battle snapshot, applied where the battler's skill set is assembled (the shared `BattleLoadout` rule, authoritative on the backend and mirrored tick-for-tick on the frontend) — so it is parity-safe like the existing dedupe/order rule and adds **no new RNG-coupled tick-loop surface**.

**Empty hands are the virtual `Unarmed` "fists"** whose signature is **punch** — an `Unarmed`-typed skill fielded only bare-handed. The equipped weapon type resolves as `weapon?.WeaponType ?? Unarmed`, so the gate is one uniform rule across real weapons and bare hands. A real `Unarmed` weapon (e.g. brass knuckles) **replaces** the fists and fields *its own* signature instead — no free bonus skill, symmetric with how a sword fields the sword's signature, and `Unarmed`-typed *selected* skills work both bare-handed and with an `Unarmed` weapon. Combined with the [no-stranding invariant](#items-item-mods-and-tags) (every weapon grants a signature fieldable with it — a martial signature matching its own type, or any non-martial one), the player **always fields ≥1 usable skill** even when every selected skill is dimmed. The gate applies **uniformly to selected and granted** skills, so it also covers the oddity of a non-weapon item that granted a weapon-typed skill (dormant unless its weapon is held).

## Skill Effects

A skill may carry any number of authored **effects**. Each effect is a timed attribute modifier with a `Target` (the caster — `Self` — or the `Opponent`), an `Attribute`, a modifier type (additive/multiplicative), an `Amount`, and a `DurationMs`. When the skill fires, each effect is applied to its target as a real attribute modifier that expires after its duration. The runtime rules (which the frontend and backend implement identically, since battle logic runs on both sides) are:

- **Magnitude can scale with a caster attribute.** An effect optionally scales off one of the **caster's** (the firing battler's) attributes: its applied magnitude is `Amount + casterAttribute × ScalingAmount`, exactly mirroring how a skill's damage scales off the caster (`BaseDamage + Σ(attribute × multiplier)`). So a poison applying `DamageTakenPerSecond` to the opponent can scale with the caster's Dexterity, and the player's own investment strengthens it. The scaling attribute is always read from the **caster**, regardless of which battler the effect lands on; a `ScalingAmount` of `0` means no scaling (the authored `Amount` is used verbatim). The magnitude is resolved once, at the moment the effect is applied — using the caster's current (possibly buffed) attributes — and is then fixed for that application's lifetime. V1 supports a single scaling attribute per effect; multi-attribute scaling is a possible later extension.

- **Damage first, then effects.** A skill computes and deals its damage from the pre-effect attribute state, then applies its effects — so a self damage-buff never boosts the very hit that carries it. Because skills resolve in loadout order, an earlier slot's effect *does* influence a later slot firing on the same tick.
- **Timed, in 40ms ticks.** A duration of `DurationMs` influences `DurationMs / 40` ticks (counting the tick it was applied on); effects expire at the start of a tick, before any skill fires.
- **Stack the magnitude, share one expiry.** Re-applying an effect stacks, so the magnitudes sum (additive amounts add, multiplicative factors compound), but every active application **on the same attribute** shares a **single expiry that resets to the newest application's duration** — there are *no* independent per-portion expirations. Following the canonical example: apply 10 DoT for 5s, then 2s later apply 20 DoT for 2s → the target now has 30 DoT for 2s (the older application is cut from its remaining 3s down to the new 2s); 1s later apply 10 DoT for 5s → 40 DoT for 5s (the whole stack adopts the new 5s). Distinct effects on the same attribute stack and share the expiry the same way; effects on **different** attributes remain independent. Stacking is unbounded (a per-effect cap is a possible later balancing lever).
- **MaxHealth never heals on a buff.** When an effect raises MaxHealth, current health is left unchanged (no free healing); when MaxHealth drops below current health, current health is clamped down to the new maximum.

Damage-over-time is **typed** — each DoT carries a damage type (bleed, poison, or burn) the attacker can amplify and the defender can resist (spike [#1320](./spikes/1320-damage-types.md)). It rides three **per-second accumulators** — `BleedDamagePerSecond`, `PoisonDamagePerSecond`, `BurnDamagePerSecond` — and heal-over-time rides one **typeless** `HealthRegenPerSecond`, all applied by an **end-of-tick phase** that runs after both battlers' skills resolve (only while both still live). The DoT type is encoded by **which accumulator an effect targets** — there is no separate type field on an effect. A poison is then pure data: a debuff that adds `PoisonDamagePerSecond` to the opponent for a duration; a regen, a self buff to `HealthRegenPerSecond`. Its rules:

- **Per-second units.** The phase applies `value × 40 / 1000` per 40ms tick, summing the DoT types in fixed order.
- **Typed: amplification frozen, resistance live.** The caster's damage-type amplification is **frozen** into the accumulator when the effect is applied (like the magnitude's caster scaling); the bearer's resistance for that type is **sampled live** each tick, so a vulnerability debuff makes existing DoTs hurt immediately. DoT still **bypasses the Toughness curve**, so type resistance is its *only* mitigation (and negative resistance — vulnerability — or resistance above 1 — absorption — fall out for free). HoT stays typeless.
- **Enemy resolves first.** The enemy's DoT/HoT is applied before the player's, so an enemy DoT kill awards victory before the player's DoT lands — a same-tick mutual DoT kill favours the player (mirroring the skill-exchange order).
- **Damage, then heal, then death.** For each battler the typed DoT applies first (**bypassing mitigation**), then `HealthRegenPerSecond` heals (capped at MaxHealth), and only then is death checked — so a heal-over-time can save a battler from an otherwise-lethal DoT tick. The enemy still resolves first (see above), so an enemy that its own same-tick regen cannot save dies before the player's DoT applies, and a same-tick mutual DoT kill still favours the player.
- **DoT is not floored at zero.** With Block's flat reduction removed, the direct-hit mitigation stack is purely multiplicative and never needs a floor either; DoT likewise goes negative only through a deliberately authored negative accumulator or a resistance above 1 (absorption) — a floor wouldn't prevent that, only mask it. Authored healing belongs in the capped `HealthRegenPerSecond` channel instead.
- **Statistics.** DoT dealt to the enemy counts toward `DamageDealt` (but not the highest-single-attack or per-skill totals — per-skill DoT attribution is deferred); DoT taken by the player counts toward `DamageTaken`; the player's post-cap healing feeds the `DamageHealed` statistic.

Effects are deterministic in this version (they always apply when the skill fires — no proc chance), and durations are time-based. Authoring effects on skills is done through the admin Workbench's skill editor.

# Proficiency

A **proficiency** is a mastery track for an **activity** — a progression layer *over* the skills you collect, not a source of them (original design in [the proficiency spike](./spikes/982-proficiency-system.md); accrual reworked in [the effect-based avenues spike](./spikes/1318-proficiency-progression-avenues.md)). Proficiencies are organized into **paths**: a path is a linear sequence of proficiency **tiers** sharing an identity arc (Fire Magic → Inferno Magic), and the next tier opens once the current one is maxed. Each path binds to a single **activity key** — a damage type, a category, or a combat event — and *performing that activity* in won battles levels its tiers (dealing its damage type, taking that type of hit, landing crits, healing, …; see [Proficiency XP](#proficiency-xp--effect-based-power-normalized-accrual)). Levels pay out permanent bonuses (and, at milestones, skills and access to deeper tiers or cross-path gateways).

Each proficiency identity is also surfaced cosmetically as a **word of power** — its name rendered in an invented, decorative arcane script. This is pure flavour (never information the player must read); the frontend implementation lives in [frontend.md](./frontend.md#conlang-script-words-of-power).

The word **deciphers as you level the tier**: it starts as undeciphered glyphs, reveals its *pronunciation* at the half-way mark, and reveals its *translation* once the tier is maxed. Each proficiency therefore carries three authored strings — the romanized `Word`, its `Pronunciation`, and its `Translation` — delivered to the client on the proficiency reference data. The two reveal thresholds are **derived, not stored**: pronunciation unlocks at `ceil(maxLevel / 2)` and translation at `maxLevel`.

## Proficiency XP — effect-based, power-normalized accrual

XP is earned **on victory only** and computed **server-side at battle completion** — it is *not* part of the deterministic battle simulation, so it carries no frontend/backend parity surface (only the eventual attribute *bonus* is parity-sensitive, computed from player state at snapshot time). Each proficiency path is a **mastery track for an _activity key_** and independently claims

`XP_path = basePie × clamp( pathActivity ÷ totalAttributes )`

routed to the path's **frontier tier** (its lowest un-maxed tier), where `pathActivity` is the battle's quantity for the path's key, `totalAttributes` is the player's power (the sum of their core additive attribute modifiers — the same measure the difficulty system uses), `basePie` is a server-only magnitude (`ServerGameConstants.ProficiencyXpPerVictory`), and the `clamp` mirrors `ServerGameConstants.MaxExpRewardMultiplier`. The claims are **independent and overlap** — there is no shared pie to split, so they need not sum to 1 (spike #1318).

- **Power-normalization is the difficulty curve.** `activity ÷ power` is a continuous difficulty ratio (a trivial enemy lets you do little relative to your power; an over-level one more), so it **subsumes** the banded `DefeatRewards` difficulty multiplier — that multiplier is **not** applied again (doing so would put power in the denominator twice and open a strip-power exploit). Growing stronger naturally outgrows old threats — a treadmill, with anti-grind for free.
- **No representation floor.** A path earns strictly in proportion to its real contribution; **commitment (build + loadout) is the only lever**. A dabbled skill doing 2% of your damage trains its path at ~2% rate — there is no "slot a 4th skill for free milestones" anti-pattern.
- **Routing by `ActivityKey`, over two books.** Each path declares one **activity key** — an offense damage type/category, a resist damage type/category, or a combat event. A battle quantity of damage type `T` trains the path(s) bound to each key `applies(T)` resolves to, on the relevant book: the **output book** (damage you dealt) routes to **offense** keys, the **incoming book** (damage that came at you) routes to **resist** keys. So a fire hit you land trains the **Fire** offense path *and* the **Elemental** offense path, while a fire hit you take trains the **Fire-resist** path *and* the **Elemental-resist** path; likewise a sword hit trains the **Swordsmanship** offense path *and* the broad **Martial** path through the shared `Physical` category, because weapon masteries are damage-type leaves under `Physical` (see [Damage types](#damage-types)). Martial skills are statically typed to their weapon (a weapon hit's identity is authored, not inherited from the equipped weapon), and weapon types add **amplification** only — there is no per-weapon resistance, so a weapon hit you take trains the **Physical-resist** path alone and physical resistance stays the single physical mitigation lever. Wired today: **offense** for the physical, elemental, weapon, and DoT leaves (typed damage dealt — direct hits and DoT alike, the latter type-routed with no source-skill attribution, so a bleed tick trains **Bleed**/**DoT**), **resistance** (per-type incoming exposure, split into the share the player's own type-resistance blocked and the share that still landed and weighted separately — full rate on the blocked share, a reduced rate on the landed share, with Toughness deliberately excluded from the split since it is a generic stat every build can raise rather than the type-specific investment a resist path represents — so a resist trains **faster** the more it actually mitigates, rather than the exposure signal being indifferent to investment — #1454), and the type-neutral combat events **crit** (Precision — trains on the **normalized marginal crit bonus**: the extra damage over a non-crit hit, saturated by `φ(a) = a/(1+a)` on crit-damage investment `a = CriticalDamage − 1`, **not** the full crit hit, so a heavier crit-damage investment trains Precision proportionally more while a single crit is bounded at one baseline hit — #1448; this is the reference tally the Hex/Momentum/Sunder/Cull archetypes copy), **dodge** (Evasion), **heal** (Restoration), **reflect** (Retribution — the reflected damage the player returns to the enemy, from the mitigation rework's reflection channel #1330), **hex** (Hex — the vulnerability-enabled marginal damage a player-applied negative-resistance debuff let through, crediting the resistance the player's own debuff removed — tracked from the applied effect, so an enemy's own resistance buff cannot rob the player of training — and booked with the same `/(1 + investment)` saturation as the crit bonus, so it is flat in the enemy's base resistance and proportional to the debuff strength — #1427, the second archetype to copy the crit reference tally), **momentum** (Momentum — the ramp-enabled marginal damage a player-applied stacking self-buff to a typed amplification attribute enabled, crediting only the buff's own contribution — tracked from the applied effect, isolated from any static amplification the player already carries — and booked with the same `/(1 + investment)` saturation on the buff's own magnitude; unlike Hex this is *not* flat in the enemy's mitigation, since Momentum amplifies the player's own output and a tougher enemy mitigates the ramp bonus like the rest of the hit — #1428, the third archetype to copy the crit reference tally), **sunder** (Sunder — the anti-mitigation counterpart to Hex, but a **designed proxy** rather than a literal marginal: since the Toughness curve is nonlinear (unlike Hex's flat resistance), there is no before/after difference through it that is actually independent of the target's own stats, so Sunder instead books `dealt × φ(investment)` directly, where the investment is the player-applied `Toughness` debuff scaled by the curve's own characteristic magnitude — reading only the player's own applied amount and level, never the defending enemy's Toughness or resistance, so a fixed investment trains the same regardless of which enemy it lands on; direct-hit only, since DoT bypasses the Toughness curve entirely — #1429, the fourth archetype to copy the crit reference tally), and **cull** (Cull — the execute-enabled marginal damage an authored `ExecuteBonus` enabled, scaled by the target's missing-health fraction at the moment of the hit — the one archetype whose enabler is a genuinely new damage-calc conditional (a real multiplier on the outgoing hit, parity-critical between the frontend and backend) rather than an existing resistance/amplification attribute, though its training signal follows the same `/(1 + investment)` saturation on the investment's own magnitude — #1430, the fifth archetype to copy the crit reference tally), which map straight to a single activity key.
- **Focus beats spread; axes train in parallel.** Damage partitions by type, so a focused loadout trains its key fully while a spread one trains each at a fraction. Across axes (offense vs. defense vs. proc) there is **no dilution** — they are independent claims — so a multi-axis build mints **more** total proficiency XP, the opportunity cost living in build investment and the per-tier curves rather than a shared pie.

Each won battle applies the gain against the frontier tier's authored XP **curve** (`BaseXp × XpGrowth^level` per level) and **cap**; a single battle can cross several levels, and a fully-maxed path has no frontier and banks nothing. The **offline-rewards** consolidation accrues identically — the same per-victory computation applied once per won battle over the away window, re-resolving the frontier and re-reading the same stationary power — so an idle session earns exactly what playing it live would. A won battle pushes its per-proficiency result to the live client; the offline path's gains ride the welcome-back summary instead.

Rarity, the per-skill home tier, and the per-skill contribution falloff are **gone** — damage already reflects a skill's power, so an explicit rarity/falloff weight would double-count (rarity reverts to display-only). The pie magnitude, the per-tier curves, and the caps are **strawman values tunable during balancing**.

## Opening tiers and milestones

A tier is **open** (trainable, and drawn on the tree) when it has accrued any XP, has the previous tier in its path maxed, or — for a cross-path **gateway** (lava = advanced-fire + advanced-earth) — has all its prerequisite tiers maxed. Openness is purely derived from progress + structure, so for a root or within-path tier *training* a path — performing the activity its key binds to (dealing its damage type, taking that type of hit, triggering its event) — is itself what opens it, and losing an item that granted a skill you trained with never closes a path you've trained. **A gateway tier is the exception: it accrues no XP until every prerequisite is maxed.** An umbrella category — e.g. *Elemental*, which the leaf damage types roll up into (`applies(Fire) = [Fire, Elemental]`) — stays locked until its leaf prerequisites are maxed, so leaf activity does **not** bank progress onto the still-locked umbrella; the routing skips a locked gateway frontier rather than letting the activity that *will* train it open it early (#1411). Authored **milestone** levels grant a **permanent reward skill** (in addition to their attribute bonus; a milestone may be bonus-only). Opening a tier (a maxed tier's successor, or a satisfied gateway) is **notification-only** — it grants no skill. A freshly-revealed tier is never walled because its **native training skill** is sourced elsewhere: a within-path tier's native is the **previous tier's max-level milestone reward**, while a cross-path or synthesized line bootstraps its native through **skill synthesis** (combine owned skills into the new line's native — superseding the former tree-seed/gateway-grant machinery). A character's starting roots are **emergent from its class kit**, not authored as universally-open: the kit's skills open their paths the first time they accrue XP (there is no "starts unlocked" flag — see [Classes](#classes)). Skills come from the starter kit, item grants, proficiency milestones, and synthesis — challenges no longer grant skills (see [Skills](#skills)).

# Classes

A **class** is chosen when a character is created and gives it a coherent starting identity — it is a rich character-creation **preset with a persistent identity hook**, not a parallel progression track. Class is **permanent**: a different archetype means a new character (the multiple-characters-per-account flow is the intended vehicle), so there is no respec or class-change machinery. Every character has a class; classes are authored **reference data** (an admin-authored catalogue, retired not deleted, like skills/items/proficiencies).

A class parameterizes what a new character looks like:

- **Starter kit** — the skills the character begins with (all selected): a weapon (carrying its signature skill via the item's granted skill) and one secondary skill for a second path. **Punch** is not a kit slot but the **virtual fists' signature** — the `Unarmed`-typed skill fielded whenever no weapon is equipped (see the [weapon-match gate](#weapon-match-gate)) — so the unarmed fallback is just the bare-handed case of the one uniform gate. It **trains the `Unarmed` (and `Martial`) mastery** in proportion to its damage like any other weapon signature; the former "path-less punch that earns no XP so it doesn't dilute the proficiency pie" carve-out is obsolete under the [effect-based accrual](#proficiency-xp--effect-based-power-normalized-accrual) (there is no shared pie to dilute, and a bare-knuckle build now trains its mastery from level one). The kit also *is* what opens the character's first proficiency paths: those skills open their paths the first time they accrue XP, so a class's starting roots are **emergent from its kit** rather than authored as universally-open (see [Opening tiers](#opening-tiers-and-milestones)).
- **Attribute fingerprint** — the class's starting attribute spread. The full model is a level-scaled, non-reallocatable **locked base** (the floor of the class's identity, `BaseAmount + AmountPerLevel × level` per the class's distribution — the same shape an enemy uses) plus a reduced **free pool** of manually-allocated stat points (a smaller per-level grant than the locked base supplies). The locked base is derived from `(class, level)` and never stored, so it re-tunes for free when growth vectors change and cannot be stripped by dumping points into one stat; only the free-pool allocations persist, and they start empty.
- **Signature passive** — a durable combat-identity bonus fed through the attribute-modifier pipeline (flat or attribute-scaled), assembled into the battler.
- **Word of power** — a decorative conlang label rendered like a proficiency's, for flavour.

The locked base and signature passive are the only *runtime* class effects; everything else a class "determines" is a value seeded once at creation. Class biases *which* proficiency paths a character starts and grows in, but never which paths are ultimately reachable — every node stays eventually reachable, and class-flavoured gear is handled softly through proficiency gear-gating, not class locks. Because the game is pre-release, introducing required classes deletes existing characters rather than grandfathering them.

# Items, Item Mods, and Tags

In early versions of this game, items dropped randomly from enemies with random modifications based on matching tags. This tag system has data like: "Weapon", "Sharp", "Two-Handed", etc. An item would have a set of tags, and item modifications would also have tags. When an item dropped, the game would roll for modifications that had matching tags to the item.

This system was ultimately scrapped in favor of a more deterministic system where items and item modifications are unlocked through Challenges. This change was made to provide a more engaging and rewarding progression system for players, as well as to allow for more strategic decision-making when it comes to character builds and playstyles. The tag system still exists as a means of determining which item modifications can be applied to which items. For example, a "Sharpened" modification may only be applicable to items with the "Sharp" tag.

An item may also carry a single optional **granted skill** (a nullable `GrantedSkillId`) that becomes an innate, always-active battle skill while the item is equipped — see [Skills](#skills) for the loadout mechanics and the deliberate player/enemy asymmetry it introduces. The target skill must be `Item`-flagged (enforced by backend authoring validation, the same intent-vs-reality bridge the other acquisition channels use). A weapon bringing its own signature ability (a staff brings *Fireball*) is the unit; multi-grant per item and set-bonus skill grants are deliberate later extensions, not part of V1.

A **weapon** item additionally carries a `WeaponType` — any damage-type leaf (reusing `EDamageType`; see [Damage types](#damage-types)) classifying what the weapon is: a martial leaf (`Sword`/`Axe`/`Bow`/`Club`/`Dagger`/`Unarmed`) for a melee weapon, or an elemental/DoT leaf for a caster weapon declaring its element. It is **only meaningful on a weapon** and is the axis a martial-typed skill matches against (and what a *"kills with a weapon type"* statistic would read). Every weapon must declare **both** a `WeaponType` and a `GrantedSkillId` — a signature attack **fieldable with that weapon** (its own type if martial, or any non-martial type) — enforced at authoring; this no-stranding invariant guarantees the player always has at least one usable skill (the deliberate tradeoff: no pure stat-stick weapons).

# Content Lifecycle — Retiring Reference Data

Authored reference content (items, item mods, skills, enemies, zones, challenges) is **retired**, never hard-deleted. Retiring takes a piece of content *out of circulation* — the game stops introducing it anew — while keeping it permanently valid for anything that already references it. This is a deliberate design choice for an idle game where content ids are baked into persisted player state (equipped items, applied mods, unlocked skills, completed challenges, battle snapshots): a player who already earned a now-retired item keeps it and it behaves exactly as before, but new players won't encounter it.

- **Retired ≠ deleted.** A retired record stays in the catalogue and is fully resolvable; it just isn't offered for new acquisition or encounters. Retiring is reversible (reinstate).
- **What "out of circulation" means today:**
  - A retired **enemy** no longer spawns in random idle encounters.
  - A retired **challenge** can no longer be completed by a player who has not already completed it — it stops accruing progress and granting its reward — and is hidden from the challenges screen unless already completed.
  - A retired **zone** is no longer navigable — the nav UI skips it (a retired zone in the middle of the order is stepped over, not a wall) and the backend refuses a change into it or a challenge against its boss. A player whose current zone is no longer *viable* (retired, or left with no spawnable enemies) is lazily **relocated** to the nearest viable, unlocked zone on their next encounter rather than being stranded.

  Already-earned content is never revoked: a completed challenge keeps its reward/unlock, and an authored reference (a zone's boss, a completed gate) still resolves by id. Admin authoring pickers likewise exclude retired records from new references, while keeping an existing authored reference visible (#295).
- **Why not delete:** deleting a non-terminal record would corrupt the id-as-index lookups the whole game relies on and orphan persisted references. See [backend.md → Retiring reference data](./backend.md#retiring-reference-data-content-lifecycle) for the mechanism and the deferred per-entity edge cases.

# Challenges and Statistics

Challenges and Statistics are relatively new additions to the game, and their design is still evolving. Challenges are a way for players to unlock new content and progression by completing specific tasks or reaching certain milestones in the game. They can range from simple objectives, like defeating a certain number of monsters, to more complex ones, like completing a zone without taking any damage.

Not all Challenges are tied to Statistics and vice versa, but many will be. Statistics are tracked metrics that can be used to gate content and progression in the game. For example, a Challenge may require a player to have a certain number of kills with a specific weapon type, which would be tracked as a Statistic.

## Battle Outcomes (`BattlesWon` / `BattlesLost` / `BattlesAbandoned`)

Every completed battle is classified by its **outcome**, and the three outcomes are mutually exclusive so they partition all battles:

- **`BattlesWon`** — the enemy died (the player won).
- **`BattlesLost`** — the player died.
- **`BattlesAbandoned`** — neither combatant died. This covers the player walking away from an unfinished fight (e.g. retreating from a boss or switching zones mid-battle) **and** the 2-minute battle timeout being reached in a stalemate (a **draw**) — both resolve as "neither died", carry no rewards, and are deliberately **not** counted as a loss since the player never actually fell (#202, #886).

The classification keys off the simulated outcome (`victory` / `playerDied`), not which code path ended the battle — a battle abandoned via `AbandonBattle` in which the player had in fact already died is still a loss, and a timeout that neither side could have survived past the cap is an abandon (a draw), not a loss. All three are recorded both globally and per-enemy. Like `BattlesLost`, `BattlesAbandoned` is a "lower is better" statistic on the Statistics screen.

The live engine enforces the cap as a real outcome (not just the display): when `timeElapsed` reaches `DEFAULT_MAX_BATTLE_MS` with both battlers alive the fight ends as a draw — the idle farm continues (a boss draw drops back to the idle loop rather than re-spawning the boss), and the unresolved battle is recorded as a draw through the same `AbandonBattle` re-simulation, which is clamped to `DefaultMaxBattleMs` so the backend replay resolves the reported stalemate identically to the client (parity) rather than running past the cap into a spurious win/loss.

## Challenge Goal Comparison Direction

Most challenges are _accumulating_ goals: the tracked statistic increases over time and the challenge is completed once it reaches **at least** the goal (e.g. "defeat 100 enemies"). However, some statistics are minimized rather than maximized — for example, `FastestVictory` records the lowest victory time, where lower is better. A `TimeTrial` challenge ("win a battle within N seconds") is therefore satisfied when the tracked value is **at or below** the goal, which is the opposite comparison. For **at or below** goals, a tracked value of `0` is treated as "no data yet" (e.g. the player has not won a qualifying battle) and does **not** complete the challenge — otherwise a brand-new player with no victories would instantly satisfy every time trial. This matches how `FastestVictory` is stored, where `0` is the sentinel for "no victory recorded".

## Zone Clears (`ZonesCleared`)

A zone is considered **cleared** when the player wins a battle started through the dedicated **"Challenge Boss"** action against that zone's single dedicated boss. The clear keys off an explicit dedicated-boss marker (`PlayerState.IsBossBattle` + the challenged `BattleZoneId`, threaded through to the battle-completed resolve), **not** the enemy's `IsBoss` flag or the player's `CurrentZoneId` — so only the challenge path clears a zone, never a boss that happens to roll out of a random spawn table. (See the boss battle-setup decision in [backend-battle.md](./backend-battle.md#battle-setup-enemy-encounter) for the dedicated-boss model itself.)

A dedicated-boss victory updates two distinct statistics, which are deliberately kept separate so each answers a different question:

- **`ZonesCleared` counts _distinct zones ever cleared_, not boss-victory events.** The per-zone entry (keyed by zone id) is a binary **"cleared" flag** that flips from `0` to `1` on the zone's first clear and stays there; the global counter increments **only** on that `0 → 1` transition. Re-farming a zone's boss therefore never re-counts the zone — clearing two different zones once each gives a global `ZonesCleared` of 2, while clearing one zone's boss fifty times leaves it at 1. A `ZonesCleared` challenge can still target either "clear any zone" (global) or a specific zone (per-zone flag).
- **`BossesDefeated` is the _farm counter_.** It increments on **every** dedicated-boss victory, tracked both globally and **per-boss** (keyed by the boss enemy's id), so a challenge can target either "defeat any boss N times" or "defeat this specific boss N times". Because per-boss rows are written, `BossesDefeated`'s declared `StatisticType.EntityType` is `Enemy` — a statistic's declared breakdown must match the rows `RecordBattleCompleted` actually writes (noted in code on `StatisticType.GetEntityType`).

This split keeps "how many zones have I beaten" and "how many times have I farmed this boss" as separate, individually meaningful numbers.

## Kills by Damage Type (`KillsByDamageType`)

Backs "kill N with `<type>`" challenges — e.g. *"kill 20 enemies with fire magic"* (#1455). A kill is attributed to whichever leaf `EDamageType` the player dealt the **most** damage of that battle (the offense book already accumulated for proficiency training), tie-broken on the lower enum ordinal — not the literal killing blow, which has no well-defined type on a multi-portion hit and would need new tracking through the tick loop for no real accuracy gain. This is a pure post-battle read (no battle-parity surface), robust to last-hit noise.

The kill rolls up through the same `Applies()` map proficiency training uses, so a Burn-majority kill books `Burn`, `Fire`, `Elemental`, and `Dot` alike — one kill counts toward every "kill with `<family>`" challenge it plausibly belongs to. Because weapon leaves are damage-type leaves ([Items](#items-item-mods-and-tags)), the same statistic also backs "kill with a weapon type" without separate plumbing. Its declared `StatisticType.EntityType` is `DamageType`, a **fixed intrinsic enum** rather than a live/retireable DB table (like `Item.WeaponType`) — a target is validated by enum membership, not retirement. Unlike the other entity-scoped statistics it writes **no global (`null`-entity) row**, so a `KillsByDamageType` challenge always requires a target damage-type key (the content lint flags one that omits it).

## Zone Progression — locking zones behind challenges

Zone navigation is gated so later zones become real progression rather than being freely reachable from the start (#190). A zone carries an optional **`Zone.UnlockChallengeId`**: it is navigable iff that field is `null` (always open) **or** the player has completed the referenced challenge.

- **Gated on a challenge, not on `Zone.Order`.** A naive "zone `N` needs zone `N-1` cleared" chain bakes a rigid, contiguous linear order into the code and is awkward to evolve. Gating on a _challenge_ instead reuses the existing challenge/unlock system (the same machinery that unlocks items and mods), decouples gating from ordering entirely, and lets any challenge gate a zone (clear the previous boss, reach a level, defeat N of something, …) — so non-linear or milestone-based progression needs no code change. The canonical setup points a zone's `UnlockChallengeId` at the previous zone's **"clear" challenge** — the one that already grants that boss's loot — so "clear a zone → its challenge completes → the next zone unlocks" falls out for free. `Order` remains purely for nav direction and display.
- **Locking is opt-in, authored content.** `UnlockChallengeId` defaults to `null`, so the mechanism ships inert: no zone is locked until a gate is authored through the admin tools (consistent with the intrinsic-vs-authored-content split — static content lives in the admin tools, not migrations). Which challenge gates which zone is therefore a content/design decision, not hardcoded logic.
- **Enforced on both ends.** The frontend gates the zone-nav arrows (a locked neighbour shows a distinct lock affordance and is not navigable) and the Zone-Cleared overlay's "Next zone unlocked" line is shown only when a clear actually flips the next zone open. The backend independently enforces the same rule as anti-cheat: a zone change (`NewEnemy`/`StartBattle`) into a locked zone is ignored (the player keeps fighting their current zone) and a `ChallengeBoss` against a locked zone is refused — so a tampered client cannot enter a zone it has not unlocked.

## Home zone — a no-combat sanctuary

A single authored **Home** zone (`Zone.IsHome`) is a place to idle *without* battling — somewhere to step away from the idle farm (to manage a build, read, etc.) without progress pressure. It is a deliberately **"fake" zone**: it never spawns enemies (and never will — authoring rejects a boss or any spawn-table membership on it) and it **never becomes the player's persisted `CurrentZoneId`** (the battle-start zone-change anti-cheat refuses a move into it). Because the persisted zone stays the player's last real combat zone, **offline rewards need no special case** — an away window replays that last zone exactly as if the player had never stepped Home (the issue's "still counted as being in the zone they last idled in").

Home is navigable like any other zone — leftmost in the nav and always open (no unlock gate) — but entering it halts the live idle loop and shows a resting state instead of a fight; it is the one zone exempt from the empty-zone relocation in [Content Lifecycle](#content-lifecycle--retiring-reference-data) precisely because the player is never actually *there* server-side. Stepping Home is a **live-only** choice: reconnecting resumes combat in the last real zone (no persisted "at home" state).

# Offline Rewards

When a player returns after being away, they are credited for the progress their idle loop *would* have made while disconnected (spike [#879](./spikes/879-offline-rewards.md)). Away time is `now − Player.LastActivity`, measured **server-side only** (never client-supplied) and anchored on battle completion. Rewards trigger only when the player was away **≥ 5 minutes**, and at most **10 hours** are ever simulated (the cap bounds both reward inflation and CPU).

- **The missed battles are fully simulated, not estimated.** A player's combat power and reward distribution are *stationary* offline — combat reads only allocations + equipment (neither changeable while away) and enemies scale with the zone, not the player — so the away window is replayed battle-by-battle (each a fresh enemy + seed, accounting `battle duration + cooldown` against the budget) rather than sampled. This is exact for discrete outcomes (per-enemy kills, challenge thresholds) and handles the boss "fight-on through losses" case naturally.
- **The simulated loop matches what the player was doing at disconnect.** The persisted `Player.AutoChallengeBoss` flag selects the loop: idle-farming the current zone, or auto-challenging that zone's boss. Offline boss-farming keeps going through losses and draws (there is no present player to drop it back to idle), which makes leaving auto-fight on against a beatable boss a deliberately lucrative — and watch-during-balancing — strategy.
- **Rewards are exp (→ levels → stat points) plus whatever challenges complete.** There are no item drops on idle kills; items/mods are unlocked only through *challenge* completions (skills come from item grants and proficiency milestones, not challenges). Exp is granted per simulated victory (so the per-grant clamp never truncates a long haul), and statistics + challenges are consolidated into a single end-of-window pass — the affected challenges are evaluated once and their rewards unlocked, with the live per-challenge notification suppressed (the welcome-back summary is the notification instead).
- **A welcome-back gate** shows the player what they earned (away duration, loop mode, battles won/lost/drawn, exp, levels & stat points gained, and the challenges completed with their unlocks) before the live idle loop starts, so simulated and live battles never overlap. A sub-threshold or empty return skips the gate. Re-anchoring `LastActivity` on claim makes an immediate re-claim a no-op, so rewards can't be double-collected by reconnecting.

## Multiple characters per account

An account can own several characters, but **only one is live at a time** (spike [#922](./spikes/922-multiple-players-per-account.md)). Switching to another character is **lossless** (up to the offline simulation cap): the character being left behind is credited for all its elapsed idle time via the same offline-rewards simulator (with the 5-minute floor dropped, since a deliberate switch should lose nothing), and the entered character runs its own welcome-back catch-up. A parked character therefore loses no progress while you play another — one-at-a-time is outcome-equivalent to running them in parallel (within the 10-hour maximum offline window), just without rendering two battles on screen at once.

# Logs

Logs here refer to messages displayed in the UI of the game for various events, such as combat actions, item drops, level-ups, and other significant occurrences. Logs are an important part of the user experience, as they provide feedback and information to players about what is happening in the game. Logs can be filtered by type (e.g., damage dealt, experience gained, level-ups) and are meant to be informative enough that the player can step away from the game and see if anything significant happened while they were away or to have a record of what happened during a battle. Logs should only ever be generated on the frontend, as they are purely a UI element and do not affect any game logic or state. The backend should not be concerned with these kinds of logs, as its primary responsibility is to maintain the integrity of the game state and logic.

Skill effects have their own log type (`SkillEffect`), distinct from raw `Damage`, so the player can toggle them independently. It covers two kinds of message, both emitted only from the live battle loop: a one-line announcement each time a timed buff/debuff is applied (every application now stacks, so each is a genuine new entry that raises the total and is worth logging), and a per-second summary of damage-over-time / heal-over-time. Because DoT/HoT applies every 40ms tick (25 times a second), it is **never logged per tick** — each over-time channel is accumulated and emitted as a single line per second (with any partial second flushed at battle end), keeping the feed readable.
